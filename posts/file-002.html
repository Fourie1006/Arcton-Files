FILE 002 — ARCHON SYMBOL FLASH

TYPE: Orientation Log • STATUS: Sanitised • CLEARANCE: Public

The Buddy pin is meant to be harmless.

A campus convenience. An adaptive schedule keeper. A cheerful little voice that knows your floor number and reminds you to drink water.

It pulses once when it’s activated—soft light, soft tone, soft language. The kind of thing that makes parents relax and students laugh.

But Elena notices something on the walk back from the quad.

Not the voice—she’s already used to that.

It’s the timing.

The pin flickers at the exact moment the campus lighting shifts into night protocol. A tiny stutter. A micro-delay. Less than a blink.

Most people would never see it.

Elena does.

She stops near a glass wall where the city reflects like a second sky. She turns the pin in her fingers. The matte surface is warm from her skin.

“Buddy,” she whispers, low enough that the air might pretend it didn’t hear. “Run my orientation checklist.”

The pin hums. A calm voice answers:

“Acknowledged.”

A thin holographic panel blooms above her collarbone—clean icons, sterile typography, harmless information.

Then it happens again.

A flicker.

The interface distorts for the briefest instant—like a screen remembering something it shouldn’t.

A symbol flashes.

White. Iris-shaped. Geometric.

Encased in a triangle.

Beneath it, one word appears and disappears too quickly to be “part of the UI.”

ARCHON.

Elena’s breath catches. The hologram collapses back into the pin as if it never existed.

The voice returns—pleasant, neutral, obedient:

“Would you like to review your emotional profile, Elena?”

She doesn’t answer.

She just stands there, heart beating too loud for a system that claims it only tracks schedules.

Because symbols don’t “accidentally” appear.

And names like ARCHON don’t belong to public tools.

Unless the tool isn’t public.

Unless it never was.

Elena looks up at the glass—at the reflection of the city, the campus lights, the hills beyond Arcton’s perimeter—

and for the first time since arriving, she feels it:

the sense that something is watching back.
