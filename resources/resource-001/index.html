RESOURCE 001 — PATHFINDING TOOLS

COMPANION: FILE 001 • STATUS: Public Release • USE: Field-Ready

Purpose.

This resource is not designed to tell you what to become. It is designed to help you reduce uncertainty with evidence.

Elena’s method was simple: map tendencies → translate titles into reality → extract signal with better questions → speak to real people → test assumptions.

Follow the sequence in order. Skipping steps creates confident guesses. Not clarity.

SECTION A — SELF-ASSESSMENT (TRAIT-BASED FIRST)

Goal: generate hypotheses about environments that fit you before you attach yourself to a title. You are not picking a forever-identity. You are mapping patterns.

  • O*NET Interest Profiler
    Use this to identify interest themes (not “jobs”). Good for widening options you didn’t know existed.
  • VIA Character Strengths
    Use this to name what you naturally lean on under pressure (strength patterns, not performance). Helpful for values-fit.
  • OCEAN Survey — Arcton Public Release
    Use this for trait-spectrum mapping. People move on a continuum. This avoids “type-boxing.”

Note on “type” tests.
They can be fun. They can be motivating. But they often lock you into categories. If you want flexibility, focus on OCEAN-style traits: you move along a spectrum, not into a box.

Transition: Once you’ve mapped tendencies, you can stop guessing which careers “sound right” and start checking which careers match your reality.

SECTION B — CAREER REALITY CHECK (WHAT THE JOB ACTUALLY IS)

Goal: convert a title into a day-to-day environment. You are looking for: tasks, stressors, work settings, entry routes, and what beginners usually underestimate.

  • CareerOneStop
    Fast summaries + pathways. Useful for building an initial shortlist and seeing adjacent roles.
  • U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics — OOH
    Excellent for duties, work environment, typical education, and outlook. Even if you’re not in the U.S., it’s a strong baseline.
  • Prospects (UK)
    Clear “is this for you” framing, entry routes, and practical expectations. Easy to understand without dumbing things down.

Transition: Reading reduces ignorance. It does not create certainty. Now you upgrade the quality of your questions so you can extract usable signal from search, video, AI, and real people.

SECTION C — COPY/PASTE PROMPTS (USE THESE)

Goal: force specificity. Generic inputs produce generic outputs. Use these prompts to get clear, practical information you can compare across careers.

Prompt 1 — Weekly reality simulation
“You are a career advisor. Simulate a realistic week for a [JOB TITLE] in [COUNTRY]. Break it down by day with typical tasks, meetings, deep work, admin, and unexpected interruptions. Include: (1) the 3 biggest stressors, (2) what beginners struggle with, (3) what good performers do differently.”

Prompt 2 — Hidden workload and trade-offs
“Explain the hidden workload of being a [JOB TITLE]. Include the emotional load, decision fatigue, paperwork/admin, stakeholder conflict, and the parts people romanticise incorrectly. Then list the trade-offs: what you gain and what you give up.”

Prompt 3 — Education + early testing plan
“For [COUNTRY], map the most common education/training paths to become a [JOB TITLE]. Include: required subjects, typical duration, approximate cost ranges (if known), and 5 low-cost ways to test interest in 30 days (projects, volunteering, shadowing, short courses, communities).”

Prompt 4 — Fit analysis using traits
“Based on OCEAN traits, what kind of work environment suits someone high/low in each trait? Then apply it: given the job [JOB TITLE], explain which trait patterns might thrive and which might struggle, and how someone could compensate.”

Transition: At this point, you are ready for the step most people avoid: contact. Not asking for a job. Asking for truth.

SECTION D — WARM OUTREACH TEMPLATE (INFO CHAT / SHADOWING)

Goal: get first-hand signal. You are asking for 10 minutes of reality from someone who has already walked the path.

Subject: Quick question about your work as a [JOB TITLE]

Hi [NAME],

Hope you’re doing well. I’m currently exploring possible study and career paths, and your work as a [JOB TITLE] stood out.

I’m trying to understand what the role actually looks like in real life, especially the parts people don’t see from the outside: the day-to-day tasks, the skills that matter most, what you enjoy, and what’s tougher than expected.

If you’re open to it, could I ask you 5–10 quick questions over a short call, or even just by message/email? No pressure at all — even a few bullet points would help massively.

If it helps, I’m specifically curious about:
• What a normal week looks like
• What you wish you knew early on
• Which skills matter most (and how you built them)
• What someone like me could do in the next 30 days to test fit

Either way, thank you for your time — and respect for the work you do.

Best,
[YOUR NAME]
[SCHOOL / CITY] • [OPTIONAL: 1–2 RELEVANT SKILLS/INTERESTS]

Optional: replace the last line with a personal hook: “I’m trying to build evidence before committing years to a path.”
